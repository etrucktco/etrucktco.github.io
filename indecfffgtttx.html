Subtract the diesel purchase cost from the electric purchase cost — the extra money you put in upfront.
OPEX savings
= €28.73 M

Total savings — lifetime (nominal)

The sticky-bar headline. Add up every year's cashflow in money-of-the-day — the inflation-adjusted operating saving plus any subsidy and the final residual value — then subtract the extra upfront investment. = 101%

ROI on incremental investment
= 205%

Same net benefit B divided by only the extra money over diesel — higher, because the diesel cost would have been spent anyway. Still a lifetime multiple, not annualized.
Avg annual OPEX saving
= €4.10 M / yr
ΔOPEX/yr = ΔOPEX ÷ y
Spread the total operating savings evenly across the contract years.
Payback period
= 2.30 years
Tp = ΔCAPEX ÷ ΔOPEX/yr
Divide the extra upfront investment by the average yearly saving — how long until the savings repay the extra cost.
Project IRR (the figure to compare with MSCI World etc.)
= 22.0% / yr
IRR solves: −ΔCAPEX + Σt=1..y CFt ÷ (1 + IRR)t = 0
The internal rate of return: the single annual compound rate at which the project's discounted cashflows net to zero. Each year's cashflow CFt is the inflation-adjusted operating saving plus any subsidy or residual. This is the only rate that is genuinely comparable to a compounded index return (e.g. MSCI World ~7.5%/yr) — unlike "ROI ÷ years", which is a simple average and overstates the annual figure.
Net present value @ discount rate d
= €11.0 M
NPV = −ΔCAPEX + Σt=1..y CFt ÷ (1 + d/100)t
Discounts every future cashflow back to today's euros at your cost of capital d, then subtracts the upfront extra investment. Positive NPV means the project creates value above simply earning the discount rate. If NPV > 0 at d = your WACC, the project beats your hurdle.

Cashflow distribution over years

The annual cashflow uses a realistic growing OPEX-saving curve (savings increase over time as diesel maintenance escalates), not a flat line. Subsidies land in years 2-4; residual value lands in the final year.

Year-t OPEX saving
ΔOPEXt = (ΔOPEX ÷ y) × [0.75 + 0.40 × (t−1)/(y−1)]
The average yearly saving is tilted: 75% of average in year 1 rising to 115% in the final year, then rescaled so the years still add up exactly to ΔOPEX.
Subsidy total
= €1.37 M
Subsidy = CAPEXe,c,gross × s/100
The subsidy amount equals the gross charger cost times the subsidy percentage. It is paid out across years 2, 3 and 4 (34% / 33% / 33%).
Residual value (final year)
= €0.96 M
Residual = Vr × N ÷ 1000
Net residual benefit per truck for the chosen period (€18k/€12k/€5k for 5/7/10 yr — lower for longer contracts because the truck is older) × N ÷ 1000.
Cumulative cashflow
Cumt = Cumt-1 + ΔOPEXt + Subsidyt + Residualt
Start at −ΔCAPEX in year 0, then each year add that year's operating saving, any subsidy, and (final year) the residual. The ✓ marks the first year the running total turns positive.

Mathematical caveats & honest limitations

  • Now investment-grade: cashflows are built year-by-year in nominal euros with inflation, then assessed with IRR and NPV — so the fleet IRR is directly comparable to a compounded index return (MSCI World ~7.5%/yr nominal).
  • IRR vs "ROI ÷ years": we use the true internal rate of return from the yearly cashflows. The old "ROI ÷ years" was a simple average that overstated the annual figure and was not comparable to compounded indices — it has been removed.
  • Inflation assumptions are yours to set: diesel typically rises faster than electricity (EU ETS-2 from 2027). Defaults: diesel +4%/yr, electricity +2%/yr, general OPEX +2%/yr. Maintenance also escalates with vehicle age (geometric profile) on top of inflation.
  • Index returns are nominal, gross: MSCI World/S&P 500 figures are long-run historical nominal averages before tax and fees; past performance is not a guarantee. The fleet IRR is also nominal and pre-tax, so the comparison is like-for-like, but neither is risk-free.
  • Belgian operator only: diesel refund, home tolls and subsidies are Belgian. Germany electric toll modeled conservatively at €0.09/km (full exemption would strengthen the case).
  • Maintenance per literature: diesel €95k/€151k/€240k (5/7/10 yr); electric set as a fraction of diesel via the scenario selector — best case 30%, TNO (NL) 50%, central 55%, ICCT (EU) 70%, conservative 80%. Battery replacement is not modeled.
  • Not financial advice: a real bank case would also model financing structure, tax (investeringsaftrek), battery degradation, and residual-value risk explicitly.

Belgium-specific calibration — what this model assumes

Every default below is set for a transport operator based in Belgium. If the operator is based elsewhere, the diesel refund, home toll rate and subsidy ceilings change and the case must be recalibrated.

  • Diesel professional refund €0.1913/L (2026) — Belgium refunds part of the excise on diesel to professional hauliers, so the model always uses the net pump price. This refund is being gradually phased out, which over time strengthens the electric case.
  • Belgian toll €0.286/km for a >32t diesel truck from 1 July 2026 (Viapass / Flanders kilometerheffing, including the new CO₂ component, up from €0.204) vs €0.038/km for a zero-emission truck — an ~€0.25/km gap that is the single biggest driver of the business case. Zero-emission is reduced, not exempt (80% infrastructure reduction in 2026).
  • Subsidy ceiling up to 70% on charging infrastructure — EU AFIF (up to 50%) stacked with the Flemish Ecologiepremie+ / VLIF; above 70% requires individual state-aid notification. Modeled as received in years 2-4, not upfront.
  • Foreign mileage is charged at each neighbouring country's own 2026 toll rate — set the per-country split in the parameters, and see the toll-rate sources block at the bottom for every figure and its source.

Discount rate / WACC — what it is and how to set it

The short version: the discount rate is the yearly return you could earn on this money elsewhere at comparable risk — your "cost of capital". It is the hurdle the project has to clear. A euro you only receive in year 7 is worth less than a euro today, and the discount rate is exactly how much less: future savings are divided by (1 + rate) for each year of waiting before being added into the NPV.

Why it matters here: it only affects the NPV — the one figure expressed in today's money. NPV discounts every future cashflow back to today at this rate, so a higher rate shrinks far-off savings more and NPV falls; a lower rate raises it. It does not change the nominal "Total savings" (that is money-of-the-day, undiscounted) and does not change the IRR. In fact the IRR is the rate at which NPV would hit exactly zero — so you compare the two: if IRR > your WACC, the project creates value.

How to set it for a Belgian transport operator:

  • WACC (weighted average cost of capital) blends what you pay for debt and the return your equity expects. If most of the fleet is financed by a bank/lease, your WACC leans toward the loan rate.
  • Typical 2026 range: 6-9% for a financed asset purchase. A truck lease at ~5-6% interest with modest equity lands near 7-8%. Use a higher figure (10-12%) if you want a stricter test or fund it mostly from equity.
  • Rule of thumb: set it to your actual financing rate plus a small buffer. If you simply want to compare against a passive index, set it to that index's expected return (e.g. 7.5% for MSCI World) and a positive NPV then means the fleet beats the index in present-value terms.

Default here is 8.0% — a reasonable mid-point WACC for a financed Belgian heavy-fleet operator in 2026. Adjust the slider to match your own financing.

Why these numbers are realistic

  • Tolls dominate the Belgian case: from 1 July 2026 a >32t diesel truck pays €0.286/km in Flanders vs €0.038/km for a zero-emission truck — at 100,000 km/yr that is a ~€25k/truck/yr toll saving alone.
  • Maintenance is period-specific: diesel €95k/€151k/€240k cumulative over 5/7/10 yr (non-linear escalation); electric is a research-based fraction of that (ICCT ~70%, TNO ~50%), selectable above.
  • Diesel price (pump vs net): the slider shows the pump price; Belgium refunds €0.1913/L excise to professional fleets in 2026 (down from €0.1924 in 2025) — calculations use the net price.
  • Subsidy range: AFIF caps at 50% of eligible infra (70% in special cases); stacked with Flemish Ecologiepremie+ / VLIF you reach 50-70%.
  • Residual & energy scale correctly with the contract length you choose.

Sensitivities you can test

  • Shift mileage to Germany: diesel tolls jump to €0.348/km, widening the gap further (electric stays low).
  • Shift mileage to France/Luxembourg: electric and diesel pay the same toll there, so the toll advantage shrinks and the case leans on fuel + maintenance.
  • Raise the diesel price or extend the contract length: both improve the electric case.
  • Switch the maintenance scenario: from conservative (80% of diesel) to best case (30%) — see how sensitive the case is to the electric-maintenance assumption.
