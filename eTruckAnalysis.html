Mathematical caveats & honest limitations

  • Now investment-grade: cashflows are built year-by-year in nominal euros with inflation, then assessed with IRR and NPV — so the fleet IRR is directly comparable to a compounded index return (MSCI World ~7.5%/yr nominal).
  • IRR is a true compound rate: the fleet's annual return comes from the internal rate of return on the yearly cashflows, so it can be placed directly next to a compounded index return (e.g. MSCI World ~7.5%/yr) on a like-for-like basis.
  • Inflation assumptions are yours to set: diesel typically rises faster than electricity (EU ETS-2 from 2027). Defaults: diesel +4%/yr, electricity +2%/yr, general OPEX +2%/yr. Maintenance also escalates with vehicle age (geometric profile) on top of inflation.
  • Index returns are nominal, gross: MSCI World/S&P 500 figures are long-run historical nominal averages before tax and fees; past performance is not a guarantee. The fleet IRR is also nominal and pre-tax, so the comparison is like-for-like, but neither is risk-free.
  • Belgian operator only: diesel refund, home tolls and subsidies are Belgian. Germany electric toll modeled conservatively at €0.09/km (full exemption would strengthen the case).
  • Maintenance per literature: diesel €95k/€151k/€240k (5/7/10 yr); electric set as a fraction of diesel via the scenario selector — best case 30%, TNO (NL) 50%, central 55%, ICCT (EU) 70%, conservative 80%. Battery replacement is not modeled.
  • Not financial advice: a real bank case would also model financing structure, tax (investeringsaftrek), battery degradation, and residual-value risk explicitly.

Cost of capital / WACC — what it is and how to set it

The short version: your cost of capital is the yearly return you could earn on this money elsewhere at comparable risk — the hurdle the project has to clear. In finance it is also called the discount rate, because it is the rate at which future euros are "discounted" back to today: a euro you only receive in year 7 is worth less than a euro now, and this rate is exactly how much less. (Note: this has nothing to do with a price discount on buying a truck — it is purely about the time-value of money.) Future savings are divided by (1 + rate) for each year of waiting before being added into the NPV.

Why it matters here: it only affects the NPV — the one figure expressed in today's money. NPV discounts every future cashflow back to today at this rate, so a higher rate shrinks far-off savings more and NPV falls; a lower rate raises it. It does not change the nominal "Total savings" (that is money-of-the-day, undiscounted) and does not change the IRR. In fact the IRR is the rate at which NPV would hit exactly zero — so you compare the two: if IRR > your WACC, the project creates value.

How to set it for a Belgian transport operator:

  • WACC (weighted average cost of capital) blends what you pay for debt and the return your equity expects. If most of the fleet is financed by a bank/lease, your WACC leans toward the loan rate.
  • Typical 2026 range: 6-9% for a financed asset purchase. A truck lease at ~5-6% interest with modest equity lands near 7-8%. Use a higher figure (10-12%) if you want a stricter test or fund it mostly from equity.
  • Rule of thumb: set it to your actual financing rate plus a small buffer. If you simply want to compare against a passive index, set it to that index's expected return (e.g. 7.5% for MSCI World) and a positive NPV then means the fleet beats the index in present-value terms.

Default here is 8.0% — a reasonable mid-point WACC for a financed Belgian heavy-fleet operator in 2026. Adjust the slider to match your own financing.

Charging infrastructure — how it is sized, costed & why these numbers

The key insight: a depot charges overnight, ~22:00–06:00 — an 8-hour window. A regional truck needs only ~420 kWh back, so it draws just ~52 kW averaged over the night; we size to ~65 kW/truck for headroom — a fraction of the truck's 400 kW maximum. Smart software staggers the trucks so the depot's simultaneous peak stays low. That is what makes it affordable: you size the grid and hardware to ~65 kW/truck, not to the nameplate.

Why overnight charging stays cheap
8 hours to put back ~420 kWh means slow, gentle charging — and software spreads the trucks so they never all pull peak power at once.
The red block is the naïve fear — every truck on full power together. The green band is reality: a low, flat load all night. The billable peak (and the Belgian capaciteitstarief) follows the green band, not the red — which is why no battery buffer is needed.

One cabinet serves many trucks. The expensive part is the central power cabinet, shared across a row of trucks: a real unit such as the Huawei DS720 (720 kW) runs ~10 trucks at once, or more across the night since they don't all need full power at the same moment. Each truck has its own cheap dispenser, but the costly electronics are pooled — which is why the cost per truck falls steeply with fleet size.

Split architecture: one expensive cabinet, many cheap plugs
The 720 kW power cabinet is the costly box. It feeds ~10 slim dispensers, one per truck — so the big cost is shared.
Add more trucks and you mostly add cheap dispensers, not whole cabinets — so the cost per truck falls as the fleet grows (≈€37k/truck at 20 trucks → ~€23k at 200).

Why this is a depot-only model. The trucks return to base and charge slowly overnight at the depot price (~€0.16–0.26/kWh). Public/fast charging is excluded — it's a separate, occasional, far pricier use case (≈€0.46/kWh, higher losses) that would distort a depot TCO. Megawatt charging (MCS) is also out of scope: it's for en-route top-ups on long-haul routes, with much heavier, costlier grid infrastructure and higher losses at megawatt power. Low-power overnight DC is both the cheapest and the lowest-loss path for return-to-base fleets.

Charging losses — why low power wins twice. Not every kWh reaches the battery: some is lost in AC/DC conversion, the transformer, cabling and standby. It depends on charging power — low-power overnight split DC ≈ 8% (≈5% conversion + 1% transformer + 1% cabling + 1% standby); high-power all-in-one ≈ 11–12% (see table below). This is a recurring cost. Example: 100 trucks, ~500 kWh/night, 300 nights, €0.16/kWh → 8% ≈ €209k/yr wasted vs ≈€327k/yr at 12% — about €120k/yr saved by staying low-power. Low-power overnight DC wins twice: lower CAPEX and lower loss.

What you pay for vs what moves the truck
You enter the wheel figure (the energy that actually drives the truck). You pay the grid for a bit more, because charging loses some on the way in.
At 1.10 kWh/km at the wheel and 8% loss, you buy 1.10 ÷ (1 − 0.08) = 1.196 kWh/km from the grid. The extra 0.096 kWh/km is the loss — it shows up as the “charging energy losses” line in the cost table. A high-power charger (12%) makes that grey slice bigger; low power keeps it small.

What "turnkey" means. The single charging-infra figure in the TCO table is the all-in, ready-to-use price — everything needed to drive a charged truck out in the morning:

  • Charging hardware — the power cabinets (the expensive electronics) and the dispensers/satellites at each parking bay.
  • Civil works — concrete foundation pads, trenching, underground cabling, bollards and wheel-stops to protect the equipment.
  • Grid connection & transformer — the medium-voltage (MV) connection and step-down transformer (see below).
  • Engineering & commissioning — design, installation, testing, and the charging-management software set-up.

Because the fixed parts (grid, transformer, engineering) are shared across all trucks, the cost per truck falls as the fleet grows — from roughly €37k/truck at 20 trucks to ~€23k/truck at 200 trucks. That is why the figure recalculates whenever you change the fleet size.

How much hardware?

  • ~1 power cabinet per 10 trucks and ~1 dispenser per truck — for pure overnight charging every truck dwells at once, so each needs its own plug. The dispensers are cheap; the power behind them is shared.
  • Higher truck-to-charger ratios exist, but only with daytime turnover (NACFE recorded US Foods on 1 charger per 3 trucks). Overnight can't do that — all trucks park at once — so this model conservatively assumes 1 dispenser/truck. Trucks returning in waves need fewer, dropping the cost further.
  • Grid: ≤5 trucks may fit the existing low-voltage connection; from ~20 trucks you need medium-voltage + transformer (Fluvius: >250 kVA is always MV).

Note on AC charging: this model is DC-only. Windrose trucks charge on DC (CCS / MCS), not AC — and DC is the right choice for a depot anyway: the AC→DC conversion happens once, in the shared cabinet, rather than being lost in every truck's on-board charger. AC depot charging is not considered.

Why we deliberately skip a battery buffer (BESS):

  • A BESS (on-site battery) earns its keep by shaving short, sharp demand peaks — which happen with fast/public charging, where lots of energy is forced through a small connection in minutes.
  • An overnight depot has the opposite profile: a long, flat, low-power load. With smart load-spreading there is almost no peak to shave, so a €0.5–2M battery would mostly sit idle. Independent modelling agrees: a 2025 Fraunhofer study found that for an overnight depot the investment in a battery storage system is not profitable regardless of capacity — its cost exceeds the savings — and NACFE/RMI warn that some fleets over-built storage they never used.
  • So BESS is excluded from the base case. It only becomes worthwhile if (a) the grid connection is hard-capped below your managed peak, (b) you add daytime fast-charging, or (c) you co-locate large solar.

And the Belgian peak-demand charge (capaciteitstarief)? Belgium bills businesses partly on their monthly peak power (maandpiek, €/kW). For naïve simultaneous charging this would be brutal — but because overnight software keeps the peak at ~65 kW/truck instead of 400 kW, the billable peak (and the charge) drops by roughly 4–6×. It becomes a normal, manageable operating cost rather than a deal-breaker — which is exactly why no battery buffer is needed to control it.

Turnkey ranges are planning-grade (±30%), triangulated from Alpitronic retail pricing (~€255/kW anchor), Smart Freight Centre, McKinsey, ICCT and 2025–2026 vendor data; high-power split systems (Huawei, Kempower, Siemens) are quote-only. These per-truck figures (≈€37k at 20 trucks falling to ~€23k at 200) sit in line with — and deliberately at the cautious end of — independent benchmarks: NACFE/RMI field data reports €14–19k per truck at scale, and the Fraunhofer/Transport & Environment depot-charging study (2025) puts infrastructure + grid connection at roughly €0.06/km for overnight (50 kW) charging. If anything this model is conservative, not optimistic. Get site-specific quotes before committing. Belgian grid context: Fluvius/VREG 2026 capacity tariffs, MV connection >250 kVA.

Depreciation / residual value — how it is modelled

At the end of the contract the trucks still have resale value. The model captures the net residual benefit of the electric fleet versus the diesel fleet (electric resale minus the diesel resale you would also have had) as a one-off cashflow in the final year. It is period-specific — a 5-year-old truck is worth more than a 10-year-old one — and you choose one of three scenarios:

Scenario5 yr7 yr10 yrRationale
Conservative€8k€4k€1kUnproven brand, thin second-hand market, fast EV tech obsolescence.
Central (default)€18k€12k€5kRealistic resale for an established e-truck with battery warranty.
Optimistic€30k€20k€10kStrong resale / warranty-backed, healthy used-EV demand.

Why electric residuals are treated cautiously: used EVs have so far depreciated faster than comparable diesels — battery packs lose ~2-3% capacity per year, and each new truck generation brings better range and faster charging, making older models feel dated. Buyers also discount for perceived battery risk. The longer you hold (10 yr), the more battery degradation and obsolescence erode the residual, which is why the figures fall steeply with the period.

Battery replacement is not modelled separately; a major pack replacement within the term would materially change these figures. Sources: MDPI / Recharged EV-depreciation studies 2025-2026.

Why these numbers are realistic

  • Tolls dominate the Belgian case: from 1 July 2026 a >32t diesel truck pays €0.286/km in Flanders vs ≈€0.057/km for a zero-emission truck — at 100,000 km/yr that is a ~€23k/truck/yr toll saving alone.
  • Maintenance is period-specific: diesel €95k/€151k/€240k cumulative over 5/7/10 yr (non-linear escalation); electric is a research-based fraction of that (ICCT ~70%, TNO ~50%), selectable above.
  • Diesel price (pump vs net): the slider shows the pump price; Belgium refunds €0.1913/L excise to professional fleets in 2026 (down from €0.1924 in 2025) — calculations use the net price.
  • Subsidy range: AFIF caps at 50% of eligible infra (70% in special cases); stacked with Flemish Ecologiepremie+ / VLIF you reach 50-70%.
  • Residual & energy scale correctly with the contract length you choose.

Sensitivities you can test

  • Shift mileage to Germany: diesel tolls jump to €0.348/km, widening the gap further (electric stays low).
  • Shift mileage to France/Luxembourg: electric and diesel pay the same toll there, so the toll advantage shrinks and the case leans on fuel + maintenance.
  • Raise the diesel price or extend the contract length: both improve the electric case.
  • Switch the maintenance scenario: from conservative (80% of diesel) to best case (30%) — see how sensitive the case is to the electric-maintenance assumption.
